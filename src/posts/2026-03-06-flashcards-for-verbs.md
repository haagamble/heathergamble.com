One of my activities for today was making flashcards for adjectives. A few days ago I made flashcards for verbs and used them in a way that got me speaking and creating a lot of sentences. So today I wanted to repeat the activity with adjectives. <!-- excerpt -->

I have stopped doing new lessons on Duolingo because the lessons have moved on to the past tense, and I have decided I need to learn the material from previous lessons before going on to something completely new. Today I reviewed the lessons for professions and adjectives. For many of them I can recognize them when I see them in a sentence, but I want to learn them better.

I chose eleven adjectives - open, closed, good, bad, easy, difficult, hot, cold, tired, beautiful, and important. These are words that I can imagine using in everyday conversations. For each flashcard I wrote the Greek word on one side. On the other side I wrote several sentences that used the word. Some sentences came from my Duolingo lessons, and others I asked ChatGPT to generate. I also wrote the opposite of each word on the back, "easy-difficult", "beautiful-ugly" etc. There is no English on the flash cards, so I need to have enough clues on the back to jog my memory if I can't remember the meaning of the word.

To use the flash cards I looked at the Greek word and then tried to say 3-4 sentences using that word. I'm still struggling a bit with this because I don't yet have a good grasp of the noun genders. After attempting my own sentences I read the sentences on the back. After reading the sentences on the back I said some more sentences using the written sentences as a guide.

One of the ways that I made new sentences was by using substitution drills. For example, on the back of the "hot" flashcard I have the sentence, "The coffee is hot." I read that sentence and then said: "The coffee is cold. The coffee is good. The coffee is bad." This was a really helpful activity. The written sentence reminded me of the gender of the noun, so choosing the correct adjective ending was easier. It was also good to say a lot of sentences in a row with the same noun. In Greek there are three different words for "the" in the singular, depending on the gender of the noun, so it was helpful to keep repeating "The coffee... The coffee... The coffee".

This turned out to be a very helpful speaking exercise. By combining flashcards with substitution drills I was able to generate a large number of sentences and get more comfortable using the adjectives. The focus was on producing sentences rather than just recognizing words.
