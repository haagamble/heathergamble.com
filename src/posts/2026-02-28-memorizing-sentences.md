One small but meaningful tool in language learning is the practice of memorizing full sentences. This should not be a large part of your strategy because real fluency means being able to create your own thoughts in the language, not just repeat phrases. <!-- excerpt --> But now and then, especially in the early stages or during a discouraging “quiet phase,” memorized sentences can give you a foothold. They help break the silence and give you the confidence to begin speaking again.

I've been in a bit of a rut doing a lot of language learning on Duolingo. I haven't been using the language out in the community. I needed to change things up a bit and get the speaking side of my brain working better. So today I spent some time working with an AI chatbot (Microsoft Copilot) to create a handful of simple personal sentences — things I might naturally say when meeting someone new. I started committing them to memory. There’s something grounding about a small set of ready‑made sentences that feel like “you,” rather than sentences from a tourist phrasebook. Mine included basics like, “I am from Scotland and my husband is from America,” and “I lived in Tajikistan for 28 years, but now I live in Greece.”

To memorize them I first asked Copilot to explain the grammar. Then I wrote the sentences in my notebook. For each sentence I practiced small parts by repeating them 10-20 times each, phrases like "from Scotland ... from America", "28 years", or "now I live". Then I practiced the whole sentence a few times. In the places where I stumbled or didn't sound very fluent I practiced those bits some more. Then I tried the whole sentence again. I worked through six sentences.

Tomorrow I plan to review them and then go out and try to use them. Maybe in a shop, maybe at a café — wherever a natural opportunity appears. The goal isn’t to rely on these sentences forever, but to let them act as stepping stones that help me get talking and break out of my rut.
